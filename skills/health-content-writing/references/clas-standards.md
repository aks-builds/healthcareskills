## What CLAS Is and Who It Applies To

The National CLAS Standards are a framework of guidance — not statute on their own — that HHS recommends for any individual or organization providing health and health services. They are widely referenced by:

- Federal health programs (HHS agencies, CMS, HRSA-funded centers)
- Joint Commission and other accreditation bodies
- State Medicaid programs
- Health plans, hospitals, and federally qualified health centers (FQHCs)
- Researchers and public-health programs

Many CLAS expectations *are* enforceable indirectly — through Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act (federal funds), state contracting language, and accreditation standards. Treat CLAS conformance as both an ethical baseline and a procurement / accreditation requirement (verify with counsel for your specific program).

---

## The Principal Standard

CLAS Standard 1 — the "Principal Standard" — sets the overall expectation: provide effective, equitable, understandable, and respectful quality care and services that are responsive to diverse cultural health beliefs and practices, preferred languages, health literacy, and other communication needs.

Every other CLAS standard supports this one. When in doubt about whether something is "CLAS-aligned," ask whether it makes care more effective, equitable, understandable, and respectful for the specific population being served.

## The 14 Enhanced Standards (by Theme)

The Enhanced Standards (Standards 2-15) cluster into three themes. Numbering and exact wording have been stable since the 2013 enhancement; verify the current text with the HHS Office of Minority Health before quoting numbered language in deliverables.

### Theme 1 — Governance, Leadership, and Workforce (Standards 2-4)

- Leadership accountability for CLAS
- Workforce that reflects the populations served
- Ongoing education and training for the workforce on CLAS, cultural humility, and language access

### Theme 2 — Communication and Language Assistance (Standards 5-8)

- Offer language assistance to individuals with limited English proficiency (LEP) and other communication needs at no cost, in a timely manner
- Inform individuals of the availability of language assistance services in their preferred language, verbally and in writing
- Ensure the competence of individuals providing language assistance (qualified interpreters and translators — not relying on family, friends, or untrained staff except in narrow emergency exceptions)
- Provide easy-to-understand print and multimedia materials and signage in the languages commonly used by the populations in the service area

### Theme 3 — Engagement, Continuous Improvement, and Accountability (Standards 9-15)

- Establish culturally and linguistically appropriate goals, policies, and management accountability
- Conduct ongoing assessments of CLAS-related activities
- Collect and maintain accurate and reliable demographic data
- Conduct regular assessments of community health assets and needs
- Partner with the community to design and implement services
- Create conflict and grievance resolution processes that are culturally and linguistically appropriate
- Communicate the organization's progress in implementing and sustaining CLAS

This list is paraphrased for working reference. Verify the exact text with the current Office of Minority Health publication before citing standards in policy documents or contracts.

## How CLAS Intersects with Section 1557

Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act prohibits discrimination based on race, color, national origin, sex, age, or disability in covered health programs. National-origin protection drives much of the operational language-access obligation:

- Meaningful access for individuals with limited English proficiency
- Qualified interpreters; restrictions on using minor children, family, or untrained staff
- Taglines / notices in the top non-English languages in the relevant state (the specific threshold and the rule itself have shifted across administrations — verify the current 1557 implementing regulation with counsel before quoting language counts or notice requirements)
- Auxiliary aids and services for people with disabilities (accessible formats, sign-language interpretation, etc.)

CLAS is the broader cultural-and-linguistic framework; Section 1557 is the federal enforcement floor for many of CLAS's language-access expectations. They are usually operationalized together.

## Implications for Patient-Facing Content

When writing or auditing patient-facing content against CLAS:

- **Audience definition** — describe the actual served population, not the national average. Demographics, languages, literacy bands, immigration / refugee context, religious / dietary practice, family structure.
- **Languages in scope** — match content to the languages most spoken by the audience, not only English and Spanish. Include indigenous languages, refugee-origin languages, and sign languages where the population warrants.
- **Reading level in each language** — score readability in the target language after translation / transcreation, not only in the English source.
- **Cultural references** — food examples, family roles, time framing ("after Friday prayers," "during Ramadan / Lent / Diwali"), age and gender norms, names and skin tones in imagery.
- **Idioms and metaphor** — strip US-only idioms ("game plan," "ballpark," "home stretch," "hit it out of the park") that translate poorly and feel exclusionary.
- **Plain language** — CLAS Standard 8 explicitly calls for easy-to-understand materials. Run readability formulas in each language (see readability-formulas.md).
- **Visual representation** — diverse skin tones, hair, hijab, kippah, turban, body sizes, ages, abilities. Test with community reviewers.
- **Health beliefs and practices** — acknowledge traditional medicine, religious practices, family decision-making structures; don't pretend they don't exist.
- **Trust and history** — some populations (Black, Indigenous, refugee, undocumented, LGBTQ+) carry valid mistrust of the health system. Avoid language that erases that or pretends it isn't there.
- **Notice of language assistance** — every patient-facing surface should make language assistance findable and free.

## Translation, Transcreation, and Interpreter Use

CLAS Standard 7 calls for competent language-assistance providers. In practice:

- **Translation** converts words from one language to another. Use for legal notices, consent forms, and clinical instructions where fidelity is essential. Use certified / sworn translators for legal-weight documents.
- **Transcreation** adapts content for culture, idiom, and reading level. Use for marketing, patient education, behavior change content.
- **Back-translation QA** — an independent translator renders the translated text back into the source language to surface meaning drift. Required for high-stakes content (consent, clinical instructions, public-health messaging).
- **Machine translation** is acceptable for triage and convenience but never for clinical or consent content without human review by a qualified translator.
- **Interpreters** — live, video, or phone — must be qualified. Do not use family members or minor children as interpreters except in narrowly defined emergency exceptions; document the exception when it occurs.

## Cultural Adaptation Beyond Language

A piece can be perfectly translated and still fail CLAS if it isn't culturally adapted. Things to check:

- **Names** — use names common in the served community for examples; avoid only Anglo names.
- **Food** — diabetes education that only discusses Western breakfast foods misses South Asian, East African, Caribbean, and Latin American audiences.
- **Family structure** — "talk to your wife" assumes a structure many audiences don't have; "talk to a family member you trust" is broader.
- **Religion** — fasting practices (Ramadan, Lent, Yom Kippur) affect medication timing, lab work, surgery scheduling.
- **Gender norms** — same-gender provider preferences, modesty during exams, decision-making roles.
- **Time framing** — "two weeks from Monday" is concrete; "in the near future" is not.
- **Imagery** — images should look like the audience.

## Auditing Content Against CLAS

A pragmatic audit pass:

1. Identify the populations the content is meant to serve. Does the content actually serve them, or only an imagined "general" patient?
2. List the languages spoken by the population. Is the content available in those languages, at the right reading level?
3. Read the content as a member of each major served population. Is anything alienating, confusing, or culturally tone-deaf?
4. Check imagery and names. Do they look like the audience?
5. Check whether language-assistance availability is communicated clearly on every entry point.
6. Run readability formulas in each language.
7. Have a community reviewer (patient, community-health worker, faith leader, school nurse — paid for their time) read the content and give feedback before publication.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Treating "translated" as "CLAS-aligned"**. Translation is necessary but not sufficient.
2. **Defaulting to English + Spanish**. The actual served population may need Vietnamese, Haitian Creole, Somali, Arabic, Mandarin, Cantonese, Hmong, Marshallese, Tagalog, Russian, Ukrainian, ASL — verify against your population.
3. **Using family members as interpreters**. Outside narrow emergency exceptions, this fails Standard 7 and Section 1557.
4. **Stock imagery that doesn't match the audience**. If every face in the content is white and able-bodied and your population isn't, the content already failed.
5. **Cultural references that exclude**. "Just like baseball..." excludes much of the world.
6. **No notice of language assistance**. Patients must know it's available and free.
7. **No measurement**. CLAS Standards 10-15 call for assessment and accountability. Audit, don't assume.

Verify all CLAS-specific guidance against the current Office of Minority Health text, and confirm Section 1557 implementing-regulation specifics with counsel before quoting in policy.
