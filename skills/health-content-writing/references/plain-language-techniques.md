# Plain-Language Techniques for Health Content

Reference for writing health content that real patients — across literacy levels, languages, and physical/emotional states — can read, understand, and act on. Examples compare weak/strong copy throughout.

## Core Techniques

The toolkit, in order of impact:

1. Active voice
2. Short sentences (target 15 words or fewer on average)
3. Common words instead of clinical jargon
4. One idea per sentence
5. Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences) with sub-headers
6. Lead with the action, then explain the why
7. Define unavoidable terms inline
8. Consistent vocabulary throughout the document
9. Numbers expressed as frequencies, not percentages or decimals
10. Pair every key instruction with a simple visual

---

## Active Voice

| Weak (passive) | Strong (active) |
|----------------|-----------------|
| The medication should be taken with food. | Take your medicine with food. |
| Patients are advised to discontinue use if symptoms occur. | Stop the medicine and call us if you feel sick. |
| Blood pressure must be monitored daily. | Check your blood pressure every morning. |
| The wound dressing is to be changed every other day. | Change the bandage every two days. |
| You will be contacted by our office. | Our office will call you. |

Active voice tells the patient who does what. Passive hides the doer and forces extra processing.

---

## Sentence Length

Aim for 15 words or fewer on average. A few longer sentences are fine; long *runs* of long sentences are not.

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| If you experience any of the symptoms listed below, including but not limited to chest pain, shortness of breath, or sudden weakness in your arms or legs, you should call 911 immediately rather than driving yourself to the emergency room. | Call 911 right away if you have any of these signs. Do not drive yourself. The signs are: chest pain, trouble breathing, sudden weakness in an arm or leg. |
| Your doctor has prescribed a new medication called metoprolol which is used to control blood pressure and should be taken every day at the same time. | Your doctor started a new medicine: metoprolol. Take it every day at the same time. It lowers your blood pressure. |

## One Idea per Sentence

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| You should take the antibiotic for the full 10 days even if you feel better, because stopping early can lead to the infection returning and may also contribute to antibiotic resistance. | Take the antibiotic for 10 full days. Do not stop early, even if you feel better. Stopping early can let the infection come back. |
| The procedure will take about an hour and you will be given anesthesia so you should arrange for someone to drive you home. | The procedure takes about an hour. You will get medicine that makes you sleepy. You will not be able to drive home. Arrange a ride. |

## Chunking and Structure

Patient-facing content benefits from visual chunks. Use:

- Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences max)
- Descriptive sub-headers ("When to call us" beats "Notifications")
- Bulleted lists for items that have no order
- Numbered lists for steps
- A boxed "What to do next" recap at the end of each major section
- A "Call us if..." section with explicit triggers, set apart visually

Avoid walls of prose. Avoid bullets that are themselves long paragraphs.

## Action-First Framing

Lead with what the patient should *do*, then explain *why*. This serves both literacy and the realistic case where a patient skims.

| Weak (why-first) | Strong (action-first) |
|------------------|------------------------|
| Because blood thinners increase bleeding risk, it is important to avoid contact sports while taking warfarin. | Do not play contact sports while you take warfarin. Warfarin makes it harder for your blood to clot, so you bleed more easily. |
| Since untreated high blood pressure can cause kidney damage, daily monitoring is recommended. | Check your blood pressure every day. High blood pressure can hurt your kidneys if it is not treated. |

## Defining Unavoidable Terms

Some clinical terms cannot be replaced — the patient needs the word to talk with the pharmacy, the lab, or a specialist. Define inline.

- "Atrial fibrillation (a fast, uneven heartbeat)..."
- "Your A1C (a blood test that shows your average blood sugar over 3 months)..."
- "Your INR (a number that shows how thin your blood is on warfarin)..."
- "Methotrexate (the medicine you take once a week for your rheumatoid arthritis)..."

Define once per document, then use the term consistently. A glossary at the end of the document or rendered inline by the portal is also acceptable.

## Consistent Vocabulary

Pick one word for each concept and stick with it.

- Pick "doctor" *or* "provider" *or* "clinician" *or* "PCP" — don't rotate.
- Pick "medicine" *or* "medication" — don't rotate.
- Pick "appointment" *or* "visit" *or* "check-up" — don't rotate.

Rotating vocabulary feels writerly but adds cognitive load. Patients re-read to check whether two words mean the same thing.

## Hedges and Anxiety Words

"May," "could potentially," "in some cases," and similar hedges are sometimes necessary (uncertainty is real). Used loosely, they erode trust and increase anxiety.

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| This medicine may possibly cause some side effects in certain patients. | Most people have no side effects. About 1 in 10 feels a little dizzy. Call us if that happens. |
| Your results could potentially indicate a concern. | Your test came back a little high. We want to check it again. Here is what that means. |
| It is generally recommended that patients consider following up. | Please book a follow-up visit in the next 2 weeks. |

If you are uncertain, be concrete about *what* is uncertain and what the next step is.

## Numeracy

Most patients struggle more with numbers than with words. Default rules:

- **Frequencies over percentages**: "About 1 in 10 people" beats "10%."
- **Consistent denominators**: do not switch between "1 in 100" and "1 in 1,000" in the same paragraph.
- **Absolute risk over relative risk**: "From 4 in 100 to 2 in 100" beats "cuts stroke risk by 50%."
- **Avoid decimals**: "about 1 in 4" beats "23.7%."
- **Concrete time horizons**: "Over the next 10 years" beats "lifetime risk."
- **Icon arrays** (100-person pictograms) make probabilities tangible; AHRQ and the Risk Science Center publish vetted examples (verify currency and license).

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| This procedure has a 0.5% mortality rate. | About 5 out of every 1,000 people who have this procedure do not survive it. We do everything we can to make this safer for you. |
| Treatment reduces relative risk by 30%. | Without treatment, about 6 in 100 people in your situation have a heart attack in 10 years. With treatment, about 4 in 100 do. |

## Warning and Risk Language

Be direct. Pair the prohibition with the reason and the alternative.

- "Do not take this medicine if you are pregnant or might be pregnant. This medicine can harm a developing baby. Call us before starting if pregnancy is possible — we have other options."
- "Do not drive for 24 hours after this visit. The medicine you got makes you sleepy and slows your reaction time. Ask a friend or family member to drive you."

Avoid double negatives ("Do not skip not taking..."). Make warning text visually distinct without relying on color alone — icon + label + sufficient contrast.

## Stigma-Free and OpenNotes-Friendly Phrasing

Notes shared with patients (now the default in most US settings under ONC information-blocking rules — verify current scope) need to read accurately *and* respectfully.

| Stigma-laden | Better |
|--------------|--------|
| The patient denies chest pain. | The patient reports no chest pain. |
| Non-compliant with insulin. | Has not been able to take insulin as prescribed; barriers include cost and refrigeration. |
| Drug-seeking. | Reports inadequate pain control on current regimen. Requests review of pain plan. |
| Obese. | BMI 32. Discussed weight, nutrition, and activity. |
| Failed therapy with SSRI. | Did not get the response we hoped for from the SSRI. |
| Substance abuser. | Has substance use disorder. |

Notes remain legal medical records. Clinical accuracy and defensibility are not optional. Do not hide or soften clinically important findings — change the framing, not the truth.

## Self-Review Checklist

Before sending content out for clinical / legal review:

- [ ] Read it out loud. Where did you stumble?
- [ ] Run at least two readability formulas. Do they roughly agree?
- [ ] Underline every clinical term. Is it defined or replaced?
- [ ] Circle every "may," "could," "consider." Are they earning their place?
- [ ] Highlight every number. Is it a frequency with a consistent denominator?
- [ ] Look at the first sentence of every paragraph. Does it tell the patient what to *do*?
- [ ] Show it to one non-clinical reader who is not a writer. Ask them to teach it back.
