# Behavior Change Frameworks for Patient Engagement

Reference for selecting a behavior-change framework deliberately, instead of defaulting to whatever is fashionable. Each framework fits some problems and not others. Verify any specific intervention you cite against the current evidence base — engagement literature evolves quickly.

## How to Choose

Match the framework to the **behavior** and the **diagnosis of what's missing**, not to the team's familiarity.

| Behavior pattern | Likely good fit |
|------------------|-----------------|
| Patient hasn't decided whether to change (smoking, weight, alcohol) | Transtheoretical / Motivational Interviewing |
| Patient hasn't acted on preventive screening (mammo, colorectal, vaccine) | Health Belief Model |
| Multi-component barriers (cost, access, knowledge, motivation all mixed) | COM-B / Behaviour Change Wheel |
| Building a small daily habit (BP cuff, blood-glucose, weight) | BJ Fogg / habit-formation |
| Long-term self-management in chronic disease | Self-Determination Theory / Social-Cognitive Theory |
| Choice architecture for a one-time decision (advance directive, organ donation, vaccine sign-up at appointment) | Nudges |

Use a framework to *diagnose* first, then *design*. Designing without diagnosis is why most engagement campaigns plateau.

## Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change)

**Concept**: People move through stages — precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance, sometimes relapse — when changing a behavior. Different stages need different messages.

**When it fits**: Behaviors with a clear "before / after" — smoking, alcohol, weight, substance use, exercise initiation. Less useful for one-off decisions.

**Design implications**:
- Diagnose the stage (a short readiness ruler — "On a scale of 0-10, how ready are you to quit smoking in the next 30 days?")
- Match the message to the stage:
  - Precontemplation: raise awareness; do not push action — pushing action here backfires.
  - Contemplation: build the pros, address the cons.
  - Preparation: concrete next-step support (quit date, NRT prescription, app sign-up).
  - Action: skill-building, reminders, social support.
  - Maintenance: relapse prevention, identity-reinforcement.
- Cohort patients by stage and route to different journeys; do not send the same message to all.

**Common misuse**: Treating all patients in a cohort as "action stage" because they enrolled in the program. Most are not there yet.

## Health Belief Model

**Concept**: Health behavior is driven by **perceived susceptibility**, **perceived severity**, **perceived benefits**, **perceived barriers**, **cues to action**, and **self-efficacy**.

**When it fits**: Preventive screening, vaccination, single-action health behaviors. Strong empirical track record for cancer screening, immunization, and STI testing campaigns.

**Design implications**:
- Map each component for the target behavior in the target population: what do they believe about *their* susceptibility? What barriers — cost, time, fear, transportation — actually stop them?
- Design content that addresses the weakest component first. If barriers dominate (cost, transport), no amount of severity-framing will move the needle.
- Cues to action: timed reminders, birthday-month outreach, post-visit follow-up.
- Self-efficacy: show that people like the patient have done this; concrete next step ("Click here to schedule" beats "Talk to your doctor").

**Common misuse**: Defaulting to fear-framing (severity) without addressing barriers and self-efficacy.

## COM-B and the Behaviour Change Wheel

**Concept** (Michie et al.): Behavior = Capability × Opportunity × Motivation. Each splits further (physical / psychological capability; physical / social opportunity; reflective / automatic motivation). The Behaviour Change Wheel maps these to intervention functions (education, persuasion, training, restriction, environmental restructuring, modelling, enablement, incentivisation, coercion).

**When it fits**: Complex, multi-barrier behaviors where it isn't obvious whether the patient *can't*, *can't easily*, or *won't*. Useful for systems-level program design.

**Design implications**:
- Diagnose which of C, O, or M is missing for each segment before choosing an intervention.
- Capability missing → education, training, skill-building.
- Opportunity missing → environmental restructuring, enablement (vouchers, transport, hours), social opportunity (peer groups).
- Motivation missing → persuasion, modelling, incentives, reflective conversation.
- Do not assume motivation is missing — it usually isn't. Opportunity is the most commonly under-diagnosed.

**Common misuse**: Picking interventions from the Wheel without doing the COM-B diagnosis first.

## BJ Fogg Behavior Model

**Concept**: B = MAP — Behavior happens when Motivation × Ability × Prompt converge above an action threshold. To make a behavior easier, raise motivation, increase ability (shrink the behavior), or improve the prompt.

**When it fits**: Tiny daily habits — taking a pill, recording a BP, doing 30 seconds of breathing exercise, logging a glucose reading. The Tiny Habits methodology builds from anchor habits the patient already does.

**Design implications**:
- Shrink the behavior. "Take your morning meds with your coffee" is easier than "build a daily medication routine."
- Anchor to an existing habit. "After you brush your teeth, take your BP" beats "remember to take your BP every morning."
- Design the prompt: the right cue at the right moment beats motivational messaging.
- Celebrate immediately after the behavior (text confirmation, streak indicator). Reinforcement matters.

**Common misuse**: Treating motivation as the lever when ability and prompt are usually the cheaper levers.

## Self-Determination Theory

**Concept** (Deci / Ryan): Sustainable behavior requires **autonomy** (sense of choice), **competence** (sense of capability), and **relatedness** (sense of connection).

**When it fits**: Long-term self-management — diabetes, hypertension, chronic kidney disease, behavioral-health treatment plans. Useful for adherence and retention beyond 6 months.

**Design implications**:
- Autonomy: offer real choices ("Would you prefer SMS reminders or a weekly phone call?"). Avoid coercive framing.
- Competence: scaffold; start with achievable milestones; show progress.
- Relatedness: care-team continuity, peer groups, community-health workers, family involvement.
- Pair-program: SDT-informed coaching often outperforms one-way reminder campaigns at 6+ months.

**Common misuse**: Coercive framing ("If you don't take your medicine you will die") undermines autonomy and tends to backfire long-term.

## Nudges (Thaler / Sunstein)

**Concept**: Choice architecture — defaults, framing, salience, anchoring, social proof — shifts behavior without restricting options.

**When it fits**: One-time or low-frequency decisions in a structured context — advance directives, organ donation, flu shot at an appointment, default to generic at pharmacy.

**Design implications**:
- **Defaults** move behavior more reliably than messaging. Opt-out flu shot at the annual visit, opt-out advance-care-planning question, opt-out generic substitution.
- **Framing**: gain-frame ("get") for prevention, loss-frame ("avoid") for risk reduction; the right frame is context-dependent.
- **Social proof**: "Most patients in your situation choose..." can backfire if the patient feels coerced or if it's not true.
- **Salience**: simplify the choice; reduce competing options.

**Ethical caveats**: Nudges in healthcare can shade into manipulation, especially for patients with low health literacy or in vulnerable states. Document the choice; review with ethics or patient advisory.

**Common misuse**: Using nudges to push patients toward provider-preferred outcomes (more imaging, more visits) rather than patient-preferred outcomes.

## Social-Cognitive Theory (Bandura)

**Concept**: Behavior change driven by **self-efficacy** (belief in one's capability), **outcome expectations**, **observational learning**, and **environmental factors**. Self-efficacy is the central construct.

**When it fits**: Skill-based behaviors — insulin injection technique, inhaler use, glucometer reading, home BP, wound care.

**Design implications**:
- Build self-efficacy through **mastery experiences** (small wins), **vicarious learning** (video of someone like the patient doing the task), **verbal persuasion** (specific praise), and **physiological feedback** (showing the BP went down).
- Use peer-modelling videos for skill demonstrations.
- Avoid generic "you can do it" — specific praise tied to the action.

**Common misuse**: Confusing self-efficacy with general confidence. Self-efficacy is task-specific; a patient may have high self-efficacy for taking pills and low self-efficacy for injections.

## Motivational Interviewing

**Concept**: Not a model so much as a conversational style. Resolves ambivalence by guiding patients to articulate their own reasons for change. Core techniques: open questions, affirmations, reflective listening, summaries.

**When it fits**: Conversations (live, video, secure-message dialogue) where the patient is ambivalent — substance use, weight, smoking, medication non-adherence rooted in mixed feelings.

**Design implications**:
- Train staff (CHWs, care managers, MAs) in MI fundamentals.
- Do not deploy MI through one-way SMS — it depends on real listening.
- Pairs well with Transtheoretical (diagnose stage, then use MI to move toward action).

**Common misuse**: "MI-flavored scripts" that ask leading questions rather than open ones.

## When Frameworks Combine

Real programs usually combine more than one:

- **CHF post-discharge**: Health Belief Model for the initial check-in messages, BJ Fogg for daily weight habit, Self-Determination Theory for the 90-day coaching arc, Motivational Interviewing for the human-callback conversations.
- **Smoking cessation**: Transtheoretical to stage, Motivational Interviewing in conversations, BJ Fogg for quit-day tactics, SDT for maintenance.
- **Preventive screening**: Health Belief Model to design the outreach content, Nudges to design the default (opt-out scheduling) at the appointment.

Pick a primary framework per program stage; let secondary frameworks inform design choices.

## When Not to Use a Framework

Some patient situations are not behavior-change problems. Don't pretend they are.

- **Structural / SDOH**: cost, transport, housing, food insecurity, immigration concerns, lack of insurance. No amount of HBM messaging fixes a $400 copay. Refer (closed-loop) and remove the barrier.
- **Clinical**: side effects, dosing complexity, missed diagnosis. Refer back to the care team.
- **Communication breakdown**: the patient didn't understand the original instructions. Fix the content (see health-content-writing), not the patient.
- **Trust deficit**: historical mistreatment, recent breach, billing dispute. Repair the relationship, not the behavior.

A framework chosen for the wrong problem makes the program look like it's the patient's fault.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Choosing a framework by what the team knows**, not by what fits the behavior.
2. **Skipping diagnosis**. COM-B without the COM diagnosis is just intervention bingo.
3. **One framework for all program stages**. Pre-action and maintenance call for different tools.
4. **Treating SDOH as a motivation problem**. It's almost always opportunity.
5. **Using nudges in vulnerable contexts without ethics review**.
6. **Generic "self-efficacy" messages**. Self-efficacy is task-specific.
7. **Not measuring**. The framework is a hypothesis. Use a control cohort to test whether the intervention actually changed behavior; verify against current evidence.
